The History of the 16 Personalities

Carl Jung's Psychological Types (1921)

The story begins with Swiss psychiatrist Carl Gustav Jung, who published 'Psychological Types' in 1921. In this landmark work, Jung proposed that human behavior is not random — it follows recognizable patterns rooted in how people prefer to perceive the world and make decisions. Jung identified two primary attitudes (Introversion and Extraversion) and four functions (Thinking, Feeling, Sensing, and Intuition), which could combine to create eight distinct psychological types.

Isabel Briggs Myers and Katharine Cook Briggs

Inspired by Jung's work, Katharine Cook Briggs and her daughter Isabel Briggs Myers spent decades researching and developing a practical psychometric tool based on Jung's theories. During World War II, Isabel was motivated by a desire to help women entering the industrial workforce for the first time find roles that suited their personalities. By the 1940s, the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) had taken shape, and it added a fourth dichotomy — Judging vs. Perceiving — to Jung's original framework.

Modern Development

The MBTI was first published for general use in 1962 by Educational Testing Service. Since then, it has become one of the most widely used personality assessments in the world, administered to over 2 million people annually. It has been used in corporate team building, career counseling, relationship therapy, academic research, and personal development.



How Personality Type is Determined

Your personality type is made up of four letters, each representing a preference on one of four dimensions. These are not rigid boxes but rather points on a spectrum that indicate your natural tendencies and preferences — not your abilities or limitations.



E or I

S or N

T or F

Scientific Validity and Criticism

The MBTI and 16 personality frameworks have generated significant scholarly discussion. Here we present a balanced view:

Supporting Evidence

Decades of real-world application in organizational settings, career counseling, and therapy

High reported usefulness among participants, with many finding deep personal resonance

The framework has catalyzed important research into cognitive functions and psychological type

Consistent patterns are observed across different cultures and populations



Criticisms and Limitations

Test-retest reliability concerns: some individuals receive different results when retested weeks later

The binary nature of the four dimensions may oversimplify a more continuous human reality

The 'Barnum effect' — descriptions may be written broadly enough that many people identify with multiple types

Limited predictive validity for specific job performance compared to other instruments like the Big Five



We encourage users to approach their personality type as a tool for self-reflection rather than a definitive label. Your type is a starting point, not a destination.

THE FOUR DICHOTOMIES — DETAILED EXPLANATION

Understanding the four dimensions is the foundation of the personality type system. Each dimension represents a spectrum between two opposite preferences. Most people will identify with one end of each spectrum more naturally, though the degree of that preference can vary widely.



DIMENSION 1: Extraversion (E) vs. Introversion (I)

This dimension, first articulated by Carl Jung, describes where you direct your primary energy and how you are energized and drained.

Extraversion (E)

Extraverts draw energy from the external world of people, events, and things. They tend to think out loud and prefer to act first and reflect later. Social interaction is energizing for them, and they often feel restless or drained by prolonged solitude.

Think by talking and processing out loud

Energized by social interaction and external stimulation

Prefer breadth of experience over depth

Tend to be action-oriented and expressive

Generally feel comfortable meeting new people

May find silence and solitude uncomfortable for extended periods



Introversion (I)

Introverts draw energy from their inner world of thoughts, ideas, and reflections. They tend to think before speaking and prefer to reflect before acting. Social interaction, especially with unfamiliar people, can be draining, and they require alone time to recharge.

Think deeply before speaking

Energized by solitude and inner reflection

Prefer depth of experience over breadth

Tend to be thoughtful and reserved

Prefer a few deep relationships over many acquaintances

May find too much social stimulation draining



IMPORTANT: Introversion is not the same as shyness. Shyness involves anxiety about social situations; introversion is simply a preference for less stimulation. Many introverts are confident, engaging people who simply recharge alone.



DIMENSION 2: Sensing (S) vs. Intuition (N)

This dimension describes how you prefer to take in information and perceive the world around you.

Sensing (S)

Sensors focus on concrete, tangible information gathered through the five senses. They trust what is real and present, value practical experience, and prefer working with established facts and proven methods.

Trust direct experience and concrete facts

Detail-oriented and precise

Grounded in the present moment

Prefer practical, real-world applications

Value tradition and established methods

Think in sequential steps



Intuition (N)

Intuitives focus on patterns, meanings, and possibilities beyond the immediate facts. They are drawn to abstract thinking, theory, and future potential, and they often make connections between seemingly unrelated ideas.

Trust patterns, hunches, and abstract connections

Big-picture and conceptual thinkers

Oriented toward the future and possibilities

Enjoy theory, symbolism, and imagination

Value innovation and novel approaches

Think in non-linear, associative ways



DIMENSION 3: Thinking (T) vs. Feeling (F)

This dimension describes how you prefer to make decisions and evaluate information.

Thinking (T)

Thinkers make decisions based on objective logic, impersonal analysis, and consistent principles. They prioritize truth and fairness over harmony and typically evaluate choices by their logical consequences.

Prioritize logic and objective analysis

Value truth and accuracy above comfort

Apply consistent principles regardless of circumstances

Comfortable with conflict if it leads to correct outcomes

May be seen as cold but are typically impartial

Critique ideas to improve them



Feeling (F)

Feelers make decisions based on personal values, empathy, and how choices will affect people. They prioritize harmony and authenticity and evaluate choices through the lens of human impact.

Prioritize values, empathy, and human impact

Highly attuned to others' emotions and needs

Value harmony and authenticity

May find impersonal logic cold or incomplete

Make decisions based on what feels right as well as what is logically correct

Skilled at understanding the emotional dimension of situations



IMPORTANT: This dimension does not measure emotional sensitivity or intelligence. Thinkers feel emotions just as deeply as Feelers — they simply tend to set them aside in decision-making. Many Feelers are highly logical; they simply weight values alongside logic when deciding.



DIMENSION 4: Judging (J) vs. Perceiving (P)

This dimension, added by Isabel Briggs Myers to Jung's original framework, describes how you prefer to orient yourself to the external world — specifically, whether you prefer closure and structure or openness and flexibility.

Judging (J)

Judgers prefer structure, planning, and decisiveness in their external lives. They like to make decisions, follow through on plans, and have a clear structure to their day and projects. 'Judging' does not mean judgmental — it refers to a preference for coming to conclusions.

Prefer planned, organized approaches

Like to make decisions and stick to them

Enjoy finishing tasks before moving on

Feel stressed by open-ended situations

Value punctuality and reliability

Work steadily toward goals



Perceiving (P)

Perceivers prefer flexibility, spontaneity, and keeping options open. They like to stay adaptable and gather more information before committing to decisions. They are energized by new information and may work in bursts rather than steadily.

Prefer flexible, adaptable approaches

Like to keep options open

Enjoy starting projects and exploring possibilities

May feel constrained by rigid plans

Work in energetic bursts, especially near deadlines

Thrive with variety and change



COGNITIVE FUNCTIONS — COMPLETE GUIDE

Beyond the four letters, the personality type system is built on eight cognitive functions — specific mental processes that people use in different ways and to different degrees. Understanding cognitive functions deepens your understanding of your type dramatically and explains why two people with similar letters can seem quite different.



The Eight Functions Overview



Se — Extraverted Sensing

Experiencing and noticing the physical world in real time; acting on immediate sensory data

Si — Introverted Sensing

Recalling and comparing past experiences; reliability through memory and tradition

Ne — Extraverted Intuition

Exploring possibilities and connections in the outer world; brainstorming and generating ideas

Ni — Introverted Intuition

Synthesizing patterns to form a single deep insight or vision; long-range forecasting

Te — Extraverted Thinking

Organizing and structuring the external world logically; implementing systems and measuring results

Ti — Introverted Thinking

Building internal logical frameworks; analyzing for accuracy and consistency

Fe — Extraverted Feeling

Attuning to and influencing others' emotions; building harmony and group cohesion

Fi — Introverted Feeling

Evaluating based on deeply held personal values; seeking authenticity and moral integrity



The Function Stack

Each personality type uses all eight functions, but with dramatically different levels of comfort, development, and frequency. Each type has a 'function stack' — a specific ordering of four primary functions:



Dominant (1st)

Your strongest, most natural function. You use this effortlessly and it defines your primary orientation to the world.

Auxiliary (2nd)

Your second strongest function, which supports and balances your dominant. It develops in your teens and twenties.

Tertiary (3rd)

Less developed; emerges more in mid-life. Can be a source of growth and, when underdeveloped, stress.

Inferior (4th)

The function you use least naturally. Under stress, it can take over in immature ways. It is also your greatest area of potential growth.



Extraverted Sensing (Se) — Detailed

Se is the function of immediate physical experience. People who lead with Se (ESTPs and ESFPs) are highly attuned to the present moment — they notice textures, sounds, smells, and visual details that others miss. They act quickly and decisively on real-time sensory data. They thrive on variety and physical engagement and often excel in athletics, performance, craftsmanship, and entrepreneurship.

Introverted Sensing (Si) — Detailed

Si is the function of memory and internal experience. People who lead with Si (ISTJs and ISFJs) have a rich internal library of past experiences that they compare to present situations. They are reliable, consistent, and deeply attached to traditions and proven methods. They notice changes from the norm and may feel uncomfortable when familiar routines are disrupted.

Extraverted Intuition (Ne) — Detailed

Ne is the function of external possibility. People who lead with Ne (ENTPs and ENFPs) constantly notice connections between ideas, people, and situations. They generate a stream of possibilities and potential interpretations. They are energized by brainstorming and novelty and may struggle to focus on a single path when so many possibilities present themselves.

Introverted Intuition (Ni) — Detailed

Ni is the function of internal pattern synthesis. People who lead with Ni (INTJs and INFJs) unconsciously process vast amounts of information to arrive at deep insights and long-range visions. They often have a sense of what will happen before it does. They are highly focused and can become almost obsessive in the pursuit of a singular vision or insight.

Extraverted Thinking (Te) — Detailed

Te is the function of external logic and organization. People who lead with Te (ENTJs and ESTJs) are natural organizers who structure the external world for efficiency and effectiveness. They are decisive, results-focused, and skilled at building systems, managing people, and implementing plans. They may be blunt and can inadvertently override others' feelings in pursuit of efficiency.

Introverted Thinking (Ti) — Detailed

Ti is the function of internal logical analysis. People who lead with Ti (INTPs and ISTPs) constantly analyze information for internal consistency and accuracy. They build sophisticated internal frameworks to understand how things work. They value precision above all and may be reluctant to share conclusions until they are absolutely certain of their accuracy.

Extraverted Feeling (Fe) — Detailed

Fe is the function of external emotional attunement. People who lead with Fe (ENFJs and ESFJs) are exquisitely sensitive to the emotional atmosphere of their environment. They naturally adjust their behavior to meet others' needs and are skilled at building group harmony. They may sacrifice their own needs for the comfort of those around them.

Introverted Feeling (Fi) — Detailed

Fi is the function of internal value evaluation. People who lead with Fi (INFPs and ISFPs) have a rich inner world of deeply held personal values and emotional responses. They evaluate everything through the lens of authenticity and moral integrity. They are intensely private about their inner emotional life and can be fiercely protective of their values.

INTJs are best suited for careers that leverage their natural strengths. Top career choices include: Data Scientist, Software Engineer, Architect, Attorney, Judge, Strategic Planner, Investment Banker, Military Officer, University Professor, Neurosurgeon.

INTJs thrive in environments that challenge their intellect and allow for autonomy. They are excellent at systems thinking, long-term planning, and identifying flaws in complex strategies. PERSONALITY TYPE COMPATIBILITY

How the 16 Types Interact

Understanding how different personality types relate to each other is one of the most practical applications of the framework. While any two types can form meaningful relationships, certain combinations tend to have natural synergies, while others face more friction and require more intentional communication.



Compatibility Principles

The Attraction of Opposites vs. The Comfort of Similarity

Research and observation suggest two competing forces in relationship compatibility. Similar types share a worldview and communication style, which creates deep understanding and comfort. Opposite types offer complementary strengths and can balance each other's weaknesses — but they require more effort and communication to bridge differences.

The Most Important Dimensions for Compatibility

S/N Dimension

Arguably the most impactful for communication. Sensors and Intuitives often talk past each other — Sensors want concrete specifics while Intuitives speak in abstractions and possibilities. Couples with the same S/N preference tend to communicate most naturally.

T/F Dimension

Significantly impacts conflict style. Thinkers approach conflict analytically; Feelers approach it emotionally. Mixed couples need to learn each other's style to prevent misunderstandings.

E/I Dimension

Affects social needs and energy. Two introverts may happily stay home every weekend; an Extravert paired with an Introvert may need to negotiate social calendars.

J/P Dimension

Impacts lifestyle and planning. Judgers want structure; Perceivers want flexibility. This can create friction around household organization, planning, and routine.



Natural Compatibility Pairs

Some pairings are noted for their natural synergy, where each type's strengths complement the other's weaknesses:

INTJ + ENFP — The Architect and Campaigner often share deep intellectual curiosity and balance each other's introversion/extraversion beautifully

INFJ + ENTP — The Advocate and Debater challenge each other intellectually while the INFJ grounds the ENTP's ideas in human values

ISTJ + ESFP — The Logistician's structure balances the Entertainer's spontaneity, creating stability with fun

INTP + ENTJ — Intellectual partnership with complementary thinking styles

ISFJ + ESTP — The Defender's warmth softens the Entrepreneur's boldness

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS (FAQ)



What is the 16 Personalities test?

The 16 Personalities test is a personality assessment based on the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) framework, which itself is rooted in Carl Jung's theory of psychological types. It classifies people into one of 16 personality types based on four dimensions: Extraversion/Introversion, Sensing/Intuition, Thinking/Feeling, and Judging/Perceiving. Each type is described by a four-letter code, such as INFJ, ESTP, or ENFP.



How accurate is the test?

The test is widely used and many people find deep resonance with their results. However, like all personality assessments, it has limitations. Test-retest reliability — getting the same result when retaking the test weeks later — is a known limitation. Results may also vary depending on your mood and life circumstances at the time of taking the test. We recommend treating your results as a starting point for self-reflection rather than a definitive truth.



Can my personality type change over time?

Your core personality type is generally considered to remain stable throughout your life. However, as you grow, develop, and face new experiences, you may find that you use some functions more or less comfortably. People often report small shifts in their results at different life stages, particularly along the J/P or E/I dimensions. Personal growth can expand your repertoire beyond your type's natural preferences.



Is one personality type better than another?

Absolutely not. Every personality type has a unique combination of strengths and challenges. No type is superior to any other. The framework is intended to foster understanding and appreciation of human diversity — not to rank or value people differently. Different situations call for different personality strengths, and a well-rounded team or society benefits from all types.



What is the rarest personality type?

The INFJ is generally considered the rarest personality type, comprising approximately 1-2% of the global population. Among men, INFJ is even rarer. The ENTJ and INTJ types are also uncommon. The most common types tend to be ISTJ and ISFJ in many populations.



Can I be between two types?

Yes, it is common to feel that you relate to qualities of more than one type. Remember that the four dimensions are spectrums — if your preference is mild, you may feel balanced between the two poles. In such cases, reading profiles for both types and seeing which resonates more deeply with your core values and habitual patterns can be more illuminating than the test score alone.



What are cognitive functions?

Cognitive functions are the specific mental processes that underlie personality types, as theorized by Carl Jung. There are eight functions: Extraverted and Introverted versions of Sensing, Intuition, Thinking, and Feeling. Each personality type uses all eight functions to varying degrees, in a specific order called the 'function stack.' Understanding your function stack adds significant depth to your understanding of your type beyond the four-letter code.



How does personality type affect relationships?

Personality type can influence communication styles, conflict approaches, social needs, decision-making, and emotional expression — all of which significantly affect relationship dynamics. Understanding both your own type and your partner's, friend's, or colleague's type can dramatically improve empathy and communication. However, any two types can form meaningful, successful relationships with awareness and effort.



Should I use my personality type to make career decisions?

Personality type can be a valuable input in career decisions, helping you identify roles that align with your natural strengths and working style. However, it should be one factor among many — also consider your skills, values, interests, educational background, and market opportunities. Many people thrive in careers that fall outside their type's 'typical' recommendations through passion, skill development, and adaptability.



What is the difference between MBTI and 16 Personalities?

The official Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) is a proprietary, psychometric instrument developed by Isabel Briggs Myers and Katharine Cook Briggs, administered under professional guidance. '16 Personalities' is a popular online framework and test inspired by the MBTI's four-letter system but is not identical to the official instrument. While both yield the same 16 type categories, methodologies and reliability may differ.



GLOSSARY OF KEY TERMS

Myers-Briggs Type Indicator — the personality assessment developed by Isabel Briggs Myers and Katharine Cook Briggs

Personality Type

A four-letter code (e.g., INFJ) representing a person's preferences on four dimensions

Cognitive Function

A specific mental process (e.g., Extraverted Intuition, Introverted Feeling) that underlies personality type

Dominant Function

The primary cognitive function in a type's function stack; used most naturally and frequently

Auxiliary Function

The second function in the stack; supports and balances the dominant

Tertiary Function

The third function; less developed, tends to emerge in mid-life

Inferior Function

The fourth function; least natural, can be a source of stress and also of significant personal growth

Shadow Functions

The four functions not in the primary stack; used less consciously, can emerge under stress

Introversion

HOW TO TAKE THE TEST — GUIDANCE PAGE

Before You Begin

Taking a personality test can be an insightful and even transformative experience if approached with the right mindset. Here are some important guidelines to help you get the most accurate and useful results:



Answer Honestly, Not Aspirationally

The most common mistake is answering based on who you want to be rather than who you actually are. Try to reflect on your habitual, natural behavior — not your behavior in extreme situations or who you are at your best or worst.

Reflect on Patterns, Not Isolated Events

Think about how you generally behave across different contexts and over time. A single event might not be representative. Ask yourself: 'Is this how I usually am?' rather than 'Have I ever done this?'

Consider Context

Some questions may feel like 'it depends.' That is normal. Try to answer based on your most natural, default preference when all other things are equal.

Don't Over-Analyze

Some people spend so much time analyzing each question that they lose track of their genuine response. Trust your first instinct unless you feel it was clearly situational.

Take It When You Are Emotionally Neutral

Your results may shift if you take the test during a period of high stress, grief, or major life change. For the most representative results, take the test when you feel relatively settled.



After You Get Your Results

Your four-letter type code is just the beginning. Here is how to make the most of your results:

Read the full profile for your type — don't just skim the strengths

Pay equal attention to the weaknesses — this is where the real growth happens

Explore the cognitive functions behind your type for deeper insight

Consider reading the profiles of the types adjacent to yours to see if one resonates more

Discuss your results with people who know you well — they may offer valuable perspective

Apply the insights to specific areas: relationships, career, personal growth

Remember: the type is a map, not the territory. You are always more complex than any framework



Type Verification

If you are unsure about your results, consider these approaches to verify your type:

Read the profiles of all four types that share three of your letters (e.g., for INTJ: INFJ, ISTJ, INTP, ENTJ) and see which resonates most deeply

Learn about cognitive functions and see which function stack feels most naturally 'you'

Consult a qualified MBTI practitioner for professional assessment and type verification

Join personality type communities and read first-person accounts of different types
